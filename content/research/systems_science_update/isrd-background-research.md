# ISRD Research Foundations

*2026 · Systems Science · Applied Research*

---

This document describes the research context that underlies all ISRD work — the theoretical lineages, empirical bodies of evidence, and applied frameworks the practice draws on. It is a living document, updated as the research base develops.

## The Cross-Domain Research Problem

ISRD was founded on a simple observation: the same structural patterns appear across radically different fields, but because science is organized by domain, no single field can see them in full. A mechanism that explains ME/CFS energy crashes also explains learning overload and organizational burnout. A framework that models ecological tipping points also models institutional collapse and physiological threshold effects.

The research agenda at ISRD is therefore cross-domain by design. The goal is not to produce research within any single discipline — it is to identify the structural patterns that connect them, and to translate those patterns into actionable tools for practitioners, researchers, and organizations working with complex problems.

## Theoretical Foundations

ISRD draws on five major theoretical traditions in systems science:

**General Systems Theory (GST)** — Bertalanffy's project of identifying universal principles of organized complexity: wholeness, emergence, hierarchy, equifinality, and open system dynamics. GST provides the ontological framework — the basic vocabulary for what a system is and how it behaves at multiple levels of organization.

**Cybernetics** — The science of control, communication, and feedback in natural and engineered systems. Wiener, Ashby, and Beer provide the core concepts: negative feedback as the engine of goal-seeking behavior, the law of requisite variety, and the insight that what a system can regulate is bounded by its internal model of what it is regulating.

**Complexity Science** — The study of emergence, self-organization, nonlinear dynamics, and attractor landscapes. Santa Fe Institute work on complex adaptive systems, edge-of-chaos dynamics, and phase transitions. Particularly relevant to biological systems, social systems, and any domain where behavior is sensitive to initial conditions or governed by multiple competing feedback loops.

**Systems Dynamics** — Forrester's methodology of modeling dynamic feedback systems using stocks, flows, delays, and nonlinear relationships. The simulation tradition: building explicit models, testing structural hypotheses, and generating counterintuitive insights about how systems behave over time.

**Information Systems Theory** — The largely overlooked fifth tradition: the structural theory developed by software engineers and systems architects working on large-scale information systems. This tradition has developed a rigorous vocabulary for how information flows, how feedback loops introduce system instability, and how architecture constrains and enables behavior — concepts directly transferable to biological, organizational, and social systems.

## Domain Research Programs

### Chronic Illness and Biological Systems

The primary medical research focus is ME/CFS (myalgic encephalomyelitis/chronic fatigue syndrome) as a case study in biological systems science. The working hypothesis: ME/CFS is not a disease with a simple pathological mechanism but a system exhibiting persistent attractor dynamics in pathological state space — maintained by reinforcing feedback loops across metabolic, neurological, immunological, and behavioral subsystems.

This framing generates specific research questions:
- What are the attractor states in ME/CFS energy regulation, and what feedback structures maintain them?
- Why do standard interventions (graded exercise, cognitive restructuring) fail or worsen outcomes — and what does this tell us about the system's structure?
- What class of intervention could shift the system's attractor landscape rather than push against its current attractor?
- What cross-scale interactions (cellular → systemic → behavioral → social) co-determine the system's stable states?

The broader research program extends this framework to other chronic conditions characterized by persistent dysregulation: chronic pain, dysautonomia, autoimmune disorders, and metabolic syndrome.

### Learning Science and Instructional Systems

The second major research domain is the science of how humans acquire and apply complex knowledge — and how instructional systems can be designed to support rather than undermine that process.

Core research questions:
- How do cognitive load, working memory constraints, and prior knowledge interact in learning complex systems material?
- What instructional architectures produce durable understanding (not just passing performance) of systems concepts?
- What is the structural difference between writing that transfers knowledge effectively and writing that merely conveys information? (See: Reader-First Writing Method)
- How does the feedback structure of an assessment system shape what learners actually learn — and what it does to them when it fails?

### Software and Information Systems

The third research area examines software systems as a domain of applied systems science — both as an object of study and as a source of transferable insight.

Software engineers who build and maintain large-scale systems develop a structural intuition about system behavior that the other traditions lack: they have direct experience with how feedback loops introduce bugs, how information flow degradation produces system failure, and how architecture shapes the space of possible behaviors. ISRD research examines how this intuition can be formalized and transferred.

Research questions include:
- What systems science concepts map cleanly onto software architecture patterns, and what can each tradition learn from the other?
- How do evolutionary design methodologies (iterative, probe-and-learn cycles) compare to predict-and-control methodologies in complex system development?
- What does AI-augmented software development do to the feedback dynamics of the development process itself?

## The Synthesis

These three domains — biological systems, learning systems, and information systems — share a structural core:

They are all **feedback-regulated systems with attractor dynamics**, operating across multiple scales, subject to delay, capable of threshold effects and collapse, and resistant to naive intervention. The principles that govern why ME/CFS is so hard to treat also govern why organizational change is so hard to sustain, and why software systems accumulate technical debt in predictable ways.

ISRD's research program is organized around this structural core — not as metaphor, but as formal claim: the same systems science tools that clarify one domain clarify all three.
