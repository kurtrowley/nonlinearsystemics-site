# Systems Practice

**Applied Track · Integration Course**

---

*This course is in development. What follows is a working description of its scope and direction.*

---

The foundation courses — General Systems Theory, Cybernetics, Complexity Science, Systems Dynamics — each illuminate a different facet of complex system behavior. Taken individually, each is powerful. Taken together, they are transformative. But synthesis requires more than exposure to all four. It requires an integrated framework that tells you *which* lens to apply *when*, and how to move fluidly between them as you work.

Systems Practice is that integration.

## The Problem This Course Addresses

Practitioners who study systems science face a recurrent challenge: the theories are taught as separate disciplines with separate vocabularies, separate methods, and separate application domains. In the field, the problems don't arrive pre-sorted by theoretical school.

A chronic illness involves feedback dysregulation (cybernetics), threshold effects and attractor shifts (complexity science), stock-and-flow dynamics in energy and immune function (systems dynamics), and emergent behavior across biological levels (GST). No single theory is sufficient. The practitioner needs all four and a way to use them together.

The same is true for organizational design, learning system development, software architecture, and research methodology.

## The Practitioner's Toolkit

Systems Practice is organized around a unified toolkit for working with complex systems across any domain:

**Diagnose** — identify system structure: boundaries, stocks, flows, feedback loops, delays, information architecture, and attractor landscape

**Model** — represent what you know about the system's structure in a form that can be tested, communicated, and refined

**Intervene** — design experiments that probe system behavior and reveal structure that cannot be inferred from observation alone

**Evolve** — use the results of each intervention to update your model and calibrate the next intervention, following evolutionary design cycles rather than predict-and-control logic

This is not a fixed algorithm. It is a posture — a way of engaging with complex systems that is simultaneously rigorous and adaptive.

## What Makes This Approach Distinct

The integration draws on two bodies of practice that are rarely combined in systems science education:

**Information systems engineering** — the discipline of building systems that process, store, route, and act on information. Software engineers who maintain large systems develop a structural intuition about system behavior — how information flows degrade, how feedback loops introduce bugs, how architecture shapes possibility. This intuition is directly transferable to biological, organizational, and social systems.

**Evolutionary research design** — the methodology of treating inquiry as a design process: formulate a model, design an experiment to probe it, observe what the system actually does, update the model, repeat. This is how complex systems reveal their structure: not through passive observation, but through structured perturbation.

The combination produces a practice that is both analytically grounded (in the formal theories) and experimentally disciplined (in how it generates and uses knowledge).
