# General Systems Theory

**Foundation Track · Core Theory**

---

General Systems Theory (GST) is the interdisciplinary study of systems in general, with the aim of discovering principles common to all complex entities. Founded by biologist Ludwig von Bertalanffy in the 1940s, GST challenged the dominant reductionist approach in science — the belief that understanding the parts is sufficient to understand the whole.

GST argued that systems across biology, physics, sociology, and engineering share structural properties that cannot be understood by analyzing components in isolation. The implication was radical: a science of systems in general is possible, and its findings transfer across every domain.

## What You Will Learn

This course establishes the conceptual foundation for all of systems science. By the end, you will be able to:

- Define a system and identify its components, boundaries, and environment
- Distinguish open from closed systems and understand the consequences
- Explain emergence, hierarchy, and wholeness as system properties
- Recognize isomorphism — the same structure in different domains
- Apply GST concepts to analyze real systems in any domain

## Core Concepts

| Concept | Brief Definition |
|---|---|
| System | A set of elements in interaction |
| Boundary | The distinction between system and environment |
| Emergence | Properties arising from interaction not present in components |
| Hierarchy | Systems nested within systems |
| Isomorphism | Structural identity across different domains |
| Equifinality | Reaching the same end state from different initial conditions |
| Open system | Exchanges matter/energy with its environment |

## In Practice

GST provides the conceptual foundation — the vocabulary and ontological commitments that make the rest of systems science coherent.

In practice, GST thinking shows up as:

- **Boundary decisions** — every analysis requires choosing what is in the system and what is environment. GST makes explicit that this choice is a design decision, not a discovery. Different boundary choices reveal different dynamics.
- **Level selection** — deciding at what level of organization to analyze a phenomenon. GST's hierarchy concept makes clear that the same system looks different at different levels, and that emergent properties only appear at the level where they exist.
- **Isomorphism as transfer** — when you recognize the same structural pattern in two different domains, GST licenses transferring understanding from one to the other. This is the foundation of cross-domain reasoning in systems practice.
- **Open system thinking** — treating every system as exchanging energy, matter, and information with its environment. Closed-system assumptions (common in engineering and economics) produce systematic errors when applied to living or social systems.

For the practitioner who moves across domains — building software systems, researching health outcomes, designing learning experiences — GST provides the common grammar that makes cross-domain pattern recognition precise rather than merely metaphorical.
