## Hierarchy

Complex systems are almost always **hierarchical** — composed of subsystems, which are themselves composed of sub-subsystems, nested to multiple levels.

A human body contains organ systems, which contain organs, which contain tissues, which contain cells, which contain organelles, which contain molecules. Each level has its own characteristic behaviors, time scales, and system properties. Each level constrains and enables the levels below and above it.

**Hierarchical thinking** means asking: at what level is this phenomenon best understood? And: what does understanding at this level require from adjacent levels?

## Feedback

**Feedback** occurs when the output of a system is routed back as input. It is the most important structural concept in all of systems science.

### Negative (Balancing) Feedback
Counteracts deviation from a goal or set point. A thermostat is the canonical example: temperature drops → heater activates → temperature rises → heater deactivates. The system seeks equilibrium.

Negative feedback is the basis of regulation, stability, and goal-seeking behavior. It is present in every living system.

### Positive (Reinforcing) Feedback
Amplifies change. More produces more; less produces less. A microphone held near its speaker produces the familiar shriek — the loop amplifies until something limits it.

Positive feedback drives growth, collapse, learning, addiction, market bubbles, and evolutionary change. Unchecked, it produces runaway behavior. In combination with negative feedback, it produces the complex dynamics of real systems.

## Nonlinearity

Linear systems respond proportionally to inputs. Double the input, double the output. Most natural systems are **nonlinear** — responses are disproportionate, thresholds exist, and small changes can produce large effects.

Nonlinearity is why:
- A small increase in stress can produce a large increase in symptom severity
- Adding one more car to a near-capacity highway can cause a cascade of traffic
- A single spark in dry conditions can become a wildfire

Linear analysis fails with nonlinear systems. This is not a technical limitation — it is a categorical one.

## Isomorphism

Bertalanffy observed that the same mathematical structures appear across radically different domains. The logistic growth equation describes bacterial populations, the adoption of technologies, and the growth of cities. The predator-prey equations describe ecosystems, but also arms races and market competition.

These structural similarities are **isomorphisms** — formal identities across different content domains. The power of systems science is that understanding the structure in one domain gives you insight into all domains that share it.
